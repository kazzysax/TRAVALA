Lisbon city guide

🤝Customs & etiquette

Tipping
Not expected but appreciated — round up or leave 5–10% for good service. No tipping at cafés.
Greetings
Two cheek kisses (right first) between friends. A handshake is fine when meeting someone new.
Dining
The couvert (bread, olives) on your table isn't free — send it back if you don't want it. Lunch is 13:00, dinner rarely before 20:00.
Dress
Smart-casual. Beachwear stays at the beach; churches expect covered shoulders.
Don't
Don't rush the meal or ask for the bill too early — it reads as rude. Flag the waiter when you're ready.

🗺️Neighborhoods

Touristy
Alfama & Baixa
Postcard Lisbon — tram 28, castle views, tiled streets. Beautiful but pricey and packed midday.
Nightlife
Bairro Alto & Cais do Sodré
Quiet by day, roaring by night. Bars spill into the streets; Pink Street is the late-night hub.
Local
Graça & Mouraria
Where Lisboetas actually live. Cheaper food, real neighborhood feel, some of the best viewpoints.
Local
Campo de Ourique
Residential, relaxed, great market food hall. Almost no tourists.

🕰️Daily rhythm

Shops
Small shops open ~10:00, many close 13:00–15:00 for lunch, then until 19:00. Malls stay open late.
Meals
Lunch 13:00–15:00, dinner 20:00–22:30. Kitchens often close between.
Quiet days
Many family-run spots close Sundays and Monday. Museums often shut Mondays.
Rush hour
08:00–09:30 and 18:00–19:30 — trams and metro get very full.

🚇Getting around like a local

Best app
Get the "Carris Metropolitana" and metro app; locals top up a rechargeable Navegante card, not paper tickets.
Paying
Contactless works on metro gates. For trams/buses, tap your Navegante card — buying onboard costs more.
Taxis
Bolt is cheaper and more reliable than street taxis. A ride across the center runs €5–8.
Unwritten rule
Stand on the right on escalators. Let people off the tram before boarding — pushing marks you as a tourist.
